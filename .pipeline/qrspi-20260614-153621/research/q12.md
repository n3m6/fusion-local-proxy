#### (A) Published Prompt Patterns for Comparative Evaluation (Judge)

##### 1. LLM-as-a-Judge with Structured Rubric
**Source**: Zheng et al. (2023), *"Judging LLM-as-a-Judge with MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena"* (NeurIPS); also documented in OpenAI's and Anthropic's prompt engineering guides.

The LLM is assigned the role of an impartial evaluator, given explicit evaluation dimensions, a rating scale, and a required structured output format (JSON). The prompt separates the evaluator's *reasoning* (chain-of-thought) from the *final verdict*.

**Example template**:
```
You are an impartial judge evaluating multiple responses to the same question.
Your task is to compare the responses and produce a structured analysis.

First, reason step by step about each response in the following dimensions:
1. Factual accuracy
2. Completeness of coverage
3. Reasoning quality

Then, output a JSON object with exactly these fields:
- "consensus": A string summarizing points all responses agree on.
- "contradictions": An array of objects with "source_a", "source_b", and "description" fields, identifying where responses directly conflict.
- "unique_insights": An array of objects with "source" and "insight" fields, capturing valuable points made by only one response.
- "blind_spots": An array of strings describing important aspects of the question that no response addressed.

Do not include any text outside the JSON object.
```

##### 2. Comparative Chain-of-Thought (Comparative CoT)
**Source**: Wei et al. (2022), *"Chain-of-Thought Prompting Elicits Reasoning in Large Language Models"* (NeurIPS); practitioner adaptation in *Anthropic's Prompt Engineering Guide* (docs.anthropic.com) and *Prompt Engineering Guide* (promptingguide.ai).

Rather than asking for immediate structured output, the prompt walks the model through a systematic side-by-side comparison before producing the final analysis. The model is instructed to list each response's claims, then compare pairwise, then synthesize.

**Example template**:
```
I will present {N} responses to the same question. For each response, first extract and enumerate its key factual claims. Then:

Step 1 — Claims inventory: List each response's claims in a table with columns: Response ID, Claim, Confidence (explicit/implicit).

Step 2 — Convergence check: Identify claims that appear in 2+ responses. These form the consensus.

Step 3 — Divergence check: For each pair of responses, flag claims that directly contradict. Note the contradiction and which response asserted what.

Step 4 — Unique contributions: For each response, note any substantive claim made by that response alone that adds value beyond the consensus.

Step 5 — Gap analysis: Review the original question. What important aspects did none of the responses address?

Finally, output the structured JSON analysis with fields: consensus, contradictions, unique_insights, blind_spots.
```

##### 3. Multi-Agent Debate / Moderator Pattern
**Source**: Du et al. (2023), *"Improving Factuality and Reasoning in Language Models through Multiagent Debate"* (arXiv:2305.14325); also Liang et al. (2023), *"Encouraging Divergent Thinking in Large Language Models through Multi-Agent Debate"*.

An LLM acts as a *moderator* that observes multiple "debaters" (the panel responses) and identifies where they converge, where they diverge, and what each side's strongest arguments are. This pattern explicitly frames disagreement as productive and instructs the model to preserve minority viewpoints rather than erasing them.

**Example template**:
```
You are a debate moderator. Multiple AI assistants have independently answered the same question. Your job is not to pick a winner but to map the intellectual terrain they collectively cover.

For each topic area touched by the responses:
- Identify the majority view (what most responses converge on).
- Identify minority or dissenting views (what only some responses assert).
- Note where responses talk past each other (addressing different interpretations of the question).

Then produce a JSON analysis with:
- "consensus": points of broad agreement across responses.
- "contradictions": explicit disagreements between specific response pairs.
- "unique_insights": valuable perspectives offered by only one or a few responses.
- "blind_spots": important dimensions of the question that all responses missed.
```

#### (B) Published Prompt Patterns for Multi-Source Synthesis

##### 1. Source-Grounded Synthesis (RAG-style Grounding)
**Source**: Lewis et al. (2020), *"Retrieval-Augmented Generation for Knowledge-Intensive NLP Tasks"* (NeurIPS); practical patterns documented in *OpenAI Cookbook* (cookbook.openai.com) and *LangChain RAG guides*.

The LLM is explicitly instructed to *only* use information present in the provided source documents, to cite sources inline for each factual claim, and to state "the sources do not contain information about X" rather than fabricating.

**Example template**:
```
You are a research synthesizer. Below are:
1. Multiple model responses (the "panel outputs") labeled Response A, B, C...
2. An analysis identifying consensus, contradictions, unique insights, and blind spots.

Your task: Produce a single coherent final answer that draws ONLY from the information present in the panel responses and the analysis. You must follow these rules:

GROUNDING RULES:
- Every factual claim in your answer must be traceable to at least one panel response or the analysis.
- Prefer claims identified in the "consensus" section of the analysis.
- When the analysis identifies a "contradiction," present both sides and note the disagreement. Do not resolve it by picking one side unless the consensus clearly favors it.
- Include "unique_insights" that add value, attributing them to their source.
- Acknowledge "blind_spots" explicitly: tell the user what the panel did not address.
- If the sources contain no information on a sub-question, say so; do not invent an answer.
- Cite sources inline using [Source: Response X] notation for each major claim.

Begin your answer:
```

##### 2. Mixture-of-Agents (MoA) Aggregation
**Source**: Wang et al. (2024), *"Mixture-of-Agents Enhances Large Language Model Capabilities"* (arXiv:2406.04692).

Multiple "proposer" models generate responses; an "aggregator" model then synthesizes them. The aggregator is prompted to integrate the best elements from each proposal while staying strictly faithful to what was proposed. A key technique is providing the aggregator with the full text of all proposals (not summaries) to prevent information loss.

**Example template**:
```
You are an aggregator AI. You have received {N} independent responses to the same user question. Your job is to synthesize them into one comprehensive, faithful answer.

RULES:
1. INTEGRATE, don't select. Draw the best elements from each response where they agree. Where they disagree, present the range of views.
2. BE FAITHFUL. Do not introduce any factual claim, statistic, or technical detail that does not appear in at least one of the provided responses. If you are unsure whether a claim is supported, omit it.
3. BE COMPREHENSIVE. Ensure your synthesis captures the full breadth of what the responses collectively cover. Do not narrow the answer to only what all responses agree on.
4. ACKNOWLEDGE GAPS. If there is an important aspect of the question that none of the responses addressed, note this at the end of your answer.

Here is the user's question: {question}

Here are the panel responses:
[Response A]: {response_a}
[Response B]: {response_b}
...

Here is a comparative analysis of the responses:
{analysis_json}

Now produce the final synthesized answer:
```

##### 3. Principle-Guided / Constitutional Synthesis
**Source**: Bai et al. (2022), *"Constitutional AI: Harmlessness from AI Feedback"* (arXiv:2212.08073); adapted for multi-source synthesis in practitioner guides such as *Anthropic's Metaprompt* documentation.

The synthesis prompt includes an explicit "constitution" or set of principles that govern how the model must behave when merging sources. Each principle addresses a specific failure mode (hallucination, false consensus, erasing minority views). The model is asked to self-critique its draft against these principles before finalizing.

**Example template**:
```
You are a synthesis engine. You will merge multiple source responses into one final answer according to the following constitutional principles:

PRINCIPLE 1 (Grounding): Every claim must be supported by at least one source response. If no source supports a claim, the claim must not appear in the final answer.

PRINCIPLE 2 (Pluralism): Where sources disagree, present the disagreement transparently. Do not fabricate a false consensus.

PRINCIPLE 3 (Attribution): Attribute distinctive claims to their source so the reader can trace provenance.

PRINCIPLE 4 (Gap honesty): Explicitly list any important aspects of the user's question that the sources do not address.

PRINCIPLE 5 (Completeness): Include the union of all non-contradictory information from all sources. Do not omit information just because it appeared in only one source.

PROCESS:
Step 1: Draft an answer that follows all principles.
Step 2: Review your draft against each principle. For any violation, revise.
Step 3: Output the final answer.
```
